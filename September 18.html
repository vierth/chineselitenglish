Chu ci 楚辭

Songs of the South

This is perhaps the most famous collection of early poetic works outside of the Classic of Poetry, which we discussed a few weeks ago.

Unlike the Classic of Poetry, the Songs of the South is a little more coherent, and reflects the local style and culture of the time (which we will get into shortly.)

While the Classic of Poetry was a reflection of courtly style and interests in the spring and autumn period (and highly respected by Confucius), the Songs of the south were a different beast altogether. It was written much later, largely during the Fourth century BC and contains a very different style of work

A man named Wang Yi put together the Chu Ci as a collection of texts during the 2nd century AD, though he claims that he was just pushing forward a book put together by Liu Xiang who worked during the first century BC. We only really have confirmation that Wang Yi was involved.

Most people think that around half of the poems were by a man named Qu Yuan, and the other half were written in similar styles across the following few hundred years

there is some debate on the exact authorship of some of the poems, but the general shape of the anthology is fairly well understood.

The Style

You may have noticed that the style of this poem is significantly different that what we've seen in class to this point. There are some interesting features that aren't as evident in translation

Remember that the Classic of Poetry was constructed mainly of four syllable lines. There are two major styles of poetry in the Chu Ci, the "Sao" style, which is similar to the what we read today, and the "song" style, which many of the other poems are examples of.

The Song style poems tend to have six characters per line, with "xi" as the fourth character, where as the Sao style poems are usually longer with seven and six syllabus with the first line always ending in "xi"

xi 兮

This character is one of the strangest things abou the poems in the Chu Ci, because it doesn't really mean anything but it instead stands in a rhytmic marker, and some people speculate it was where music would have been allowed to go wild during the performing of a poem (because remember that these poems would often be performed).

Eventually this xi was dropped, and much later poetry was pentasyllabic, so critics argue this was an important locus of the stylistic development of Chinese poetry. The songs turned into the pentasyllabic poems and the sao style turned into the fu or the rhapsody, which tended towards prose.

Point of View

Interestingly, the Lisao is all told from the first person perspective, and the in the voice of a woman. The use of the first person becomes a trope of the genrea nd early all Sao style poems are as such. They also discuss purity against corruption.

Interpretation

Many confucian scholars of the time insited that EVERYTHING in this poem is alegorical and represents the realtionship between a minister and his king, though most modern commentors really doubt this

Sima Qian compares it with the Classic of Poetry: "The Songs of the States are sensual without being Licentious; the Lesser Ya poems are plantive without being seditious. Li Sao may be said to combine these to virtues."
