The Book/Classic of Odes/Songs 詩經

This is the oldest collection of poetry extant from China, and really the oldest thing that most would consider truely literature that we talk about in this class. It contains 305 poems which were originally accompanied with music, although this has long been lost. These poems come from a long time frame (9th century to 7th century BC) As it stands, for many years people believed that Confucius had an important hand in editing this collection and by all indications he believed that they contained important guidance for how to act.

It is also useful to consider how works get reinterpreted over the years: in early days, people read these poems politically and historically: we should act like the heros in these and not like the vilians. As you will probably have noted in reading the footnote, later scholars prefered to view these as literary and not really political (though there is a close connection between the literary and political throughout Chinese Literature.

Temple Hymns (Eulogies) 頌

Structurally the classic is divided into two (or four) sections depending on how you divide it.

The largest portion of the text (160 poems, though they are shorter than the other sections) are the Airs of the States, the guo feng. These are mostly folk songs and scholars see them as reflective on common culture.

The Lesser and Greater Ya are sometimes called court hymns and may have been ritual songs.

The Eugologies are unsprisingly eulogies and were often perfomred at court as part of sacraficial rites.
